## Elevator Pitch

> A cooperative dark fantasy board game where 1-4 players venture through a card-drawn dungeon of Angels to defeat God, using dice placement and a layered equipment system that grows more complex — and more interesting — as it takes damage.

## Core Fantasy

You are a prisoner of Heaven. The Angels who guard you are beautiful and terrible. God waits at the bottom of the dungeon. And somewhere between your starting equipment and whatever you've assembled by the final encounter, you built something that feels *wrong* in exactly the right way — a combination of degraded armor layers and attached item modifiers that your divine gift makes unfair, and you made it happen on purpose.

The core fantasy is the broken build: the moment your choices compound into something the game's designers intended but couldn't fully predict. Not luck. Not grinding. You saw the intersection before anyone else did.

---

## Unique Hook

Like Dicey Dungeons, AND ALSO your armor layers reveal new powers when they take damage — and the items you collect along the way rewrite the rules of what you have, making the build a living system that evolves for the entire session.

## Visual Identity Anchor

**Direction: "Woodcut Witness"**

**Visual rule**: Every line was cut by hand by someone who was afraid of what they were drawing.

**Supporting principles**:
1. *Speed is a visual value* — silhouette and contrast carry all information before text loads. Design test: if a card requires more than 2 seconds to categorize from across a table, the art has failed this principle.
2. *Degradation is visible in the material* — equipment layers look like misregistered overlapping prints; the history of the card is in the art itself. Design test: if the layer-reveal mechanic doesn't read visually before a player reads the card text, the art direction needs adjustment.
3. *God breaks every rule exactly once* — he is the only card using both faction colors simultaneously, with bilateral symmetry, complete and uncracked. Design test: if anything else in the game looks like the God card, the God card has lost its power.

**Color philosophy**: Black on natural cream stock. One accent color per faction: deep red-brown (Angels, encounters, danger); muted gold-yellow (boons, treasure, rest). Red appears nowhere except the God card and death conditions. No midtones — hard edges only. The restriction is the palette.

*Art Director verdict (AD-CONCEPT-VISUAL): APPROVE. Risk noted: woodcut discipline demands a specialist illustrator. Flag in artist search criteria.*

## Player Experience Analysis (MDA Framework)

### Target Aesthetics (What the player FEELS)

| Aesthetic | Priority | How We Deliver It |
| ---- | ---- | ---- |
| **Challenge** (mastery) | 1 | Dice placement decisions, build optimization, encounter timing |
| **Fellowship** (cooperative) | 2 | Shared damage routing, party build puzzle, cooperative wins |
| **Discovery** (secrets, emergence) | 3 | Non-obvious item+equipment synergies, degradation reveals |
| **Expression** (creativity, builds) | 4 | Build variety, divine gift personalization, damage targeting choices |
| **Fantasy** (make-believe) | 5 | Prisoners fighting celestial Angels to defeat God |
| **Sensation** (sensory pleasure) | 6 | Tactile dice rolling, card revelation, physical component interaction |
| **Narrative** | N/A | No narrative arc — events are atmospheric, not story-driven |
| **Submission** | N/A | Not a relaxation game |

### Key Dynamics (Emergent player behaviors)
- Players experimentally take damage to a specific equipment slot to reveal the layer beneath — strategic self-harm as a build tool
- Players debate aloud whose equipment should absorb a hit, based on what each player knows is underneath
- Players share "broken build" discoveries with other players after sessions
- Players study the Angel board between turns to predict the next middle-die outcome and plan accordingly

### Core Mechanics (Systems we build)
1. **Dice placement** — Roll 2D6 together; assign each die to an equipment slot to activate its effect
2. **Equipment degradation** — Losing Integrity removes the top equipment card, revealing the layer beneath; layers are distinct and meaningful, not uniformly better
3. **Item modification** — Items attach to equipment and modify their rules, costs, or effects; stacking items creates emergent power
4. **Angel resolution** — Enemy rolls 3D6, eliminates highest and lowest, keeps the middle; each face value maps to a specific Angel action on the board
5. **Events deck** — Shared countdown structure; each card is an event (boon, bad event, or encounter); last card is the boss

## Player Motivation Profile

### Primary Psychological Needs Served

| Need | How This Game Satisfies It | Strength |
| ---- | ---- | ---- |
| **Autonomy** | Dice allocation, damage routing, ability ordering, and build direction are all player-owned decisions with no dominant "correct" answer | Core |
| **Competence** | Knowing which combos are strong, when to strategically tank damage, and when to use the divine gift are learnable skills with visible payoff | Core |
| **Relatedness** | Cooperative damage routing creates shared investment in each player's build; a win belongs to the table, not one player | Supporting |

### Player Type Appeal (Bartle Taxonomy)

- [x] **Achievers** — finding and optimizing builds, perfecting the run — How: build depth rewards repeated play and systematic experimentation
- [x] **Explorers** — discovering hidden synergies, understanding what's under each equipment layer — How: non-obvious item+equipment intersections reward curiosity
- [x] **Socializers** — cooperative play, shared decisions, collective wins — How: damage routing and coordinated actions require table communication
- [ ] **Killers/Competitors** — not served: no PvP, no individual scoring

### Flow State Design

- **Onboarding curve**: The 2-die player pool limits initial cognitive load. The Events deck teaches through variety — players encounter each mechanic type before the system becomes complex. Divine gifts act as an emergency valve for new players.
- **Difficulty scaling**: Angel Integrity and ability complexity increase through the Event deck. The God card demands full party coordination.
- **Feedback clarity**: Dice placement results are immediate and visible. Equipment degradation is a physical, tactile event at the table. Item synergies produce visible output changes.
- **Recovery from failure**: Death ends the session, but sessions are 60-90 minutes. Replay investment is low. A lost run immediately generates "next time I'll prioritize X" thinking.

## Core Loop

### Moment-to-Moment (30 seconds)
Roll 2D6. Look at your three equipment slots. Decide which die goes where, in what order, knowing that the Angel's action is already committed. Every roll presents a genuine decision — no die result leaves you helpless, because your build was designed with this moment in mind.

### Short-Term (5-8 minutes)
A full combat encounter: Angel resolves its middle-die action, players place dice, player actions resolve first (in party-chosen order), Angel actions resolve. Repeat until one side runs out of Integrity. The pivot moment — a player loses Integrity and reveals a new equipment layer — is the drama engine inside each fight. "What's under this card?" drives the 5-minute rhythm.

### Session Level (60-90 minutes)
Draw Event cards and resolve them until the deck runs out. Good events (treasure, rest) provide items and recovery. Bad events deal direct damage or impose conditions. Encounter events trigger combat. The deck is a countdown timer — players feel the dungeon getting shorter with every card. The boss is always the last card.

### Long-Term Progression
S-Layers is a single-session game. No cross-session progression is intended or designed. Within-session growth happens through degradation and item collection — the character becomes more interesting by changing, not by accumulating. Each session is a complete arc.

### Retention Hooks
- **Curiosity**: What's under this equipment card? What do these two items do together?
- **Mastery**: I know what I should have done differently. Next run I'll build for that synergy from the start.
- **Discovery**: Sharing the broken combo you found with your table after the session ends.
- **Investment**: "If we survive this encounter, the build comes online." Stakes within the session.

## Game Pillars

### Pillar 1: Agency Over Dice
Every roll presents a genuine decision. No die result should make the player feel helpless.

*Design test*: If a mechanic works the same regardless of what the player does — regardless of how they built their equipment or what divine gift they chose — it fails this pillar.

### Pillar 2: The Build is Never Finished
Equipment degrades and reveals new layers; items rewrite the rules on what you have. Depth comes from the intersection of these two systems, not from either one alone.

*Design test*: If a player can "solve" their build in the first combat and coast the rest of the session — or if every piece of degraded equipment feels like a uniform upgrade rather than a change — the system isn't creating enough ongoing decisions.

### Pillar 3: Fast and Focused
No mechanic is allowed to slow the table. Every rule must earn its 30 seconds.

*Design test*: If a new rule adds more than one extra lookup or question at the table per encounter, cut it or fold it into an existing system.

### Pillar 4: Cooperative Ownership
A win is a shared construction. The party's builds are a collective puzzle, not parallel singleplayer games.

*Design test*: If a player could make the same choices without any information about their teammates' boards, the game has failed this pillar.

### Pillar 5: Combos Must Be Discoverable
The deepest builds emerge from non-obvious intersections — synergies that reward players for exploring the system, not for following instructions.

*Design test*: If a synergy is printed on a card, included in the rulebook, or explained in the tutorial, it doesn't count as earned discovery. Every intended broken combo must require the player to make a connection the game never explicitly telegraphed.

### Anti-Pillars (What This Game Is NOT)

- **NOT separate hit point tracking** — Integrity IS the health system. A parallel HP track would compromise *Fast and Focused*.
- **NOT permanent cross-session progression** — Within-session arc is the whole story. Persistent unlocks would compromise *The Build is Never Finished* by solving it outside the session.
- **NOT solo mode rules** — The cooperative dynamic is load-bearing, not optional. Designing around it would compromise *Cooperative Ownership*.
- **NOT narrative branching choices** — Story debates at the table are time sinks that compromise *Fast and Focused*.
- **NOT asymmetric hidden-information roles** — Shared puzzle requires shared visibility. Hidden roles would compromise *Cooperative Ownership*.

*Creative Director verdict (CD-PILLARS): CONCERNS resolved. Pillar 5 rewritten. Final set APPROVED.*

## Risks and Open Questions

### Design Risks
- **Dice economy balance** *(HIGH)* — 3D6 middle-die distribution weights heavily toward 3-4; Angel faces 1 and 6 will rarely trigger. Must validate with Monte Carlo simulation before art investment.
- **Encounter timing** *(MEDIUM-HIGH)* — Item+equipment stacking adds lookup time. The 5-8 minute target requires strict iconography discipline and playtest validation with a stopwatch from session one.
- **Damage targeting analysis paralysis** *(MEDIUM)* — "Which equipment takes the hit?" is meaningful but could slow the table. Mitigate with slot flavor (Head=utility, Body=survival, Weapon=offense) to make choices strategic rather than mathematical.

### Production Risks
- **Woodcut illustrator** *(HIGH)* — Small artist pool, 6-12 months of full-time work at $15k-40k. Must be contracted by month 4 or art direction must change. Consider woodcut treatment for Angels/God only (15-20 cards) with iconographic treatment for Event and equipment cards.
- **Fulfillment and manufacturing** *(HIGH)* — Freight/VAT/pick-and-pack is 30-50% of KS gross post-2022. MOQ at 2,000+ units for viable per-unit pricing. Get manufacturing quotes before setting the KS price.
- **Rulebook quality** *(MEDIUM)* — Cooperative games with stacked mechanics need exceptional rulebooks. Budget for a professional rulebook editor ($1,500-3,000).

### Market Risks
- **First-time solo publisher with no prior audience** *(HIGH)* — Requires 6-12 months of audience building before KS launch. Target: 1,500 mailing list subscribers + 500 BGG followers.
- **Saturated cooperative board game market** *(MEDIUM)* — Differentiation through the Dicey Dungeons + EtDC fusion is clear, but the category is crowded on Kickstarter.

### Open Questions
- Does the dice economy balance? → Monte Carlo simulation (1 week) before MVP is finalized
- Does combat hit 5-8 minutes with items in play? → MVP prototypes with stopwatch from session one
- Does woodcut art read at 63x88mm? → Test grayscale roughs at print size before commissioning full art
- Self-publish vs. publisher partnership? → Decide before KS campaign planning begins

## MVP Definition

**Core hypothesis**: Players find the dice placement + equipment degradation + item modification loop engaging for 30-90 minute sessions, and combat encounters resolve in 5-8 minutes.

**Required for MVP**:
1. 2 character boards with 3 equipment slots and 3 degradation layers per slot
2. 1 angel board with middle-die action resolution (all 6 die faces assigned)
3. 15 event cards (mix of boons, bad events, encounters, and 1 placeholder boss)
4. 18 equipment cards (3 layers × 6 card types across all 3 slots)
5. 10 item cards (representative modifier set — at least 3 synergistic pairs)
6. 2 divine gifts (one per character, meaningfully different in kind)

**Explicitly NOT in MVP**:
- Full character roster (4-6 characters)
- Multiple angel types (8+)
- God card (placeholder used)
- Woodcut art (paper prototype quality throughout)
- Full item and equipment sets
- Polished rulebook or campaign-ready components
